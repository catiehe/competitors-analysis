**Styles with 1–2 variants (41 styles):**
These are almost entirely bundles (e.g. "Women's 2 Pack // Long Sleeves"), multipacks (3-pack socks, 5-pack boxer briefs), and gift cards. They exist for AOV/gifting purposes, not as real product investment. Do not count these when assessing assortment breadth.

**Depth pattern conclusion:**
- Top 5 styles = 824 SKUs = **18.6% of total assortment**
- The math is clear: Unbound has 2–3 hero products per gender that get unlimited color and size investment, and everything else is secondary.
- Their strategy is not "wide assortment" — it's **build a small number of perfect products and rotate color constantly** to keep the catalogue feeling fresh without adding new styles.
- The travel pants inseam offering is their most customer-centric decision — it signals they take fit seriously on their #1 revenue driver.

## PART 2: WHAT WE CAN LEARN & OUR CLASSIFICATION

### A. Key Learnings from Unbound Merino

**On Assortment Strategy:**
- Their model is **depth over breadth** — 163 styles but 27 SKUs average per style. A focused assortment with heavy variant investment per style works for premium basics.
- Women's leads in style count but men's leads in avg depth — men's travel pants is their most developed product. For our brand: identify 1–2 hero products early and invest in color/size depth for those.
- Accessories and outerwear are almost token — 1 bag, 2 jacket styles. These are not their business. For us: don't overextend into categories the customer doesn't come to merino for.

**On Pricing:**
- 77% entry tier means their business runs on $80–$180 basics. The "premium merino" positioning is mostly narrative — the actual assortment is priced like a mid-market brand.
- Men's average price ($145) is $23 higher than women's ($122) — men seem willing to spend more per piece. Opportunity to price men's slightly higher.
- Almost nothing above $340 — the premium tier is effectively empty. If we want to go luxury, there's space above them.

**On Color:**
- 175 colors sounds impressive but signals a lack of discipline. No clear drop strategy, no color identity.
- Neutrals + earth tones = 59% of SKUs. Their customer is practical and neutral-preferring.
- Women's palette is warmer (Ivory, Heather Earth), men's is cooler (Charcoal, Navy). This split makes sense and we should mirror it.

**On Sizing:**
- Women's doesn't go above XL — a real gap. Men's offers full XXL. Whoever added men's XXL made the right call.
- Numeric sizing for pants only — good approach, don't overextend numeric sizing to tops.

### C. Price Positioning Recommendation

- **Position at parity or 5–10% above Unbound** in core categories — their prices are already mid-market for merino, not truly premium
- **Target sweet spots:** Women's tops at $89–$109, Women's pants at $185–$225, Men's tee at $89–$109, Men's travel pants at $195–$238
- **Avoid sub-$65** unless it's accessories/socks — low price signals low quality for merino
- **Premium opportunity:** If product quality supports it, position knitwear/outerwear at $280–$380 (above Unbound's near-empty premium tier)

---

### D. Color & Size Recommendations

**Starting Color Palette (12 colors):**
- Neutrals (must-have): Black, Charcoal, Ivory, Navy
- Earth (differentiation): Olive, Forest, Tan/Camel
- Seasonal (story): 2–3 rotating colors per season (e.g. Dusty Rose, Slate Blue, Burnt Orange)
- Total: 4 core + 3 earth + 3–5 seasonal = ~10–12 colors for launch

**Size Run:**
- Women's: XS – XXL (go one size beyond Unbound — this is a differentiator)
- Men's: S – XXL + numeric waist 28–36 for pants
- Do not offer 3XL at launch unless brand positioning is explicitly size-inclusive
